What Is AI?

History, concepts, and your first chat

AI: A Brief History

  • 1950 — Alan Turing: 'Can machines think?'
  • 1956 — Term 'Artificial Intelligence' coined
  • 1997 — IBM Deep Blue beats chess world champion
  • 2012 — Deep Learning revolution begins
  • 2022 — ChatGPT launches, changes everything
  • Today — AI is in your phone, car, and apps

The pattern:

Every few years AI gets dramatically better — not because the ideas changed, but because we got more data and more compute.

The components in that machine? Same hardware type that runs modern AI.

What AI Is (and Isn't)

  • ❌ NOT a robot with feelings
  • ❌ NOT magic or sentient
  • ❌ NOT thinking like a human
  • ✅ Pattern matching on massive data
  • ✅ Predicting the most likely next word
  • ✅ Math — a LOT of math — done very fast

Real example:

Your phone's autocomplete is baby AI. You type 'Happy birth...' and it predicts 'day' — it saw that pattern millions of times.

ChatGPT / our AI Helper does the same thing, just with billions of words and way more compute.

CPU: The Brain

  • Central Processing Unit
  • 4–16 powerful cores (modern desktop)
  • Does one complex task at a time — extremely fast
  • Good at: decisions, branching logic, running programs
  • Clock speed: 3–5 GHz (billions of cycles/sec)
  • The expert who can solve any problem but works solo.

Why CPU isn't enough for AI:

Training a neural network means multiplying millions of numbers, millions of times.

A CPU does this one at a time. Even at 5 GHz — too slow.

There's a better tool for this →

GPU: The Muscle

  • Graphics Processing Unit
  • 1,000–16,000+ smaller cores
  • Thousands of simple math operations simultaneously
  • Originally for rendering pixels (same math: matrix multiply)
  • AI training = matrix multiplication at massive scale
  • The 3,000 interns who can only do addition — but all at once.

The NVIDIA H100 (used for ChatGPT):

• 16,896 CUDA cores
• 80 GB VRAM
• $30,000+ per card

Meta used 24,000 of them to train Llama 3.

The GPU in your old machine? Maybe 512 cores. Same idea, different scale.

The Key Difference

  • CPU: One expert accountant who can solve any problem — works on one thing at a time.
  • GPU: 3,000 interns who can only do basic addition — but they ALL work at the same time.
  • AI math = a LOT of addition happening in parallel.
  • So for AI: you want the 3,000 interns.
  • → That's why GPU VRAM is the #1 spec for an AI machine.

How AI Learns

Training, inference, and the Code Helper

Training vs Inference

  • 🏋️ TRAINING:
  • Show the model billions of examples
  • Adjust millions of 'weights' until predictions improve
  • Takes weeks, costs millions, needs hundreds of GPUs
  • Only happens once (per model version)
  • ⚡ INFERENCE:
  • Use the trained model to answer questions
  • What we're doing right now on our laptops
  • Much cheaper and faster than training

Analogy:
Training = going to school for years
Inference = answering a question on the test

The model file on your laptop:

Prompt Engineering

Talk to AI like a pro

"Garbage in, garbage out." The quality of your AI answer depends entirely on the quality of your question.

Anatomy of a Good Prompt

  • 1. ROLE — who should the AI be?
  • 'Act as a patient coding tutor...'
  • 2. CONTEXT — what do you have?
  • 'I have a quiz game with a questions array...'
  • 3. TASK — what do you need?
  • 'Write the function that shows each question...'
  • 4. FORMAT — how do you want the answer?
  • 'Explain each line in a comment.'
  • 5. CONSTRAINTS — any limits?
  • 'Keep it simple — I'm just learning.'

You don't need all 5 every time.

But the more you include, the better the answer.

The simplest rule:
Be specific about what you have, what you need, and who you are.

Most students go from bad to great prompts just by adding their skill level to every question.

Prompt Techniques That Work

  • 'Explain this like I'm [level]' — calibrates depth
  • 'Here's what I have so far: [code]. Now add [feature].' — gives context
  • 'That's close but I need X instead of Y' — iterate, don't restart
  • 'What does line 5 do?' — ask about specific parts
  • 'Can you write a simpler version first?' — scaffolded help
  • 'What would go wrong if I did X?' — prediction and reasoning

Reassembly Order — Why It Matters

  • Step 1: CPU → Step 2: Thermal paste + cooler → Step 3: RAM
  • Step 4: Storage (SATA/M.2) → Step 5: GPU
  • Step 6: Front panel connectors (hardest part!)
  • Step 7: PSU main power (24-pin) + CPU power (4 or 8-pin)
  • Step 8: Case fans → Step 9: Cable management → Step 10: Close case
  • Most common mistakes:
  • — RAM not fully seated (must hear/feel both clicks)
  • — Front panel power connector on wrong pins
  • — PSU switch on back still OFF
  • — GPU power cable not fully plugged in

The Trickiest Part: Front Panel

  • PWR SW — power switch
  • RESET SW — reset button
  • HDD LED — drive activity light
  • POWER LED+ / LED— — power indicator
  • The manual is your best friend here.
  • Labels are tiny, on the board edge.
  • If it doesn't power on, 90% of the time:
  • A — wrong front panel connector
  • B — power cable not fully seated

How to read the manual:

Look for 'JFP1' or 'F_PANEL'.
It shows a tiny pin diagram.

Pro tip:
Plug in power switch first. If the machine POSTs, worry about the rest after.

IF IT POSTS → YOU WIN. 🏆

AI Ethics

Real questions, no right answers

🤖 Scenario 1: The Hiring Algorithm

A company trains AI on 10 years of hiring history. The data shows most successful hires came from 3 universities and were mostly male. The AI learns this — and starts filtering out everyone else, even qualified candidates.

💬 Discuss:
  • Who is responsible — the AI, the company, the engineers, or all of them?
  • Can you 'fix' the AI without fixing the underlying data?
  • Should companies be required to audit their AI before using it for hiring?

📱 Scenario 2: What Does Your Phone Know?

Your phone has: location history, microphone access, camera, all messages, search history, who you call and how long, sleep patterns, every app you open and when. All of this trains personalization AI.

💬 Discuss:
  • Did you actually agree to this? (Did you read the Terms of Service?)
  • Is 'free in exchange for data' a fair deal?
  • What would you want to change if you could?

🎨 Scenario 3: AI Art — Who Owns It?

Stable Diffusion (which we used yesterday) was trained on billions of images scraped from the internet — including copyrighted artwork. Artists now see AI generate images 'in their style' without credit or payment. You made images with it.

💬 Discuss:
  • Is training on copyrighted art fair use or theft?
  • Do you own the images you generated? Does the artist whose style it mimics?
  • What should the law say?

💼 Scenario 4: Jobs — AI Replaces and Creates

AI is already doing parts of jobs in writing, customer service, coding, data entry, and medical diagnosis. New jobs appeared: AI trainers, prompt engineers, AI safety researchers, model evaluators.

💬 Discuss:
  • Which jobs do you think are safe from AI in 10 years? Why?
  • What skills do you think will still matter?
  • Is this different from when factories replaced manual labor?

GPU Removal & Examination
Intermediate · Hardware

The GPU is the part that runs AI models. Remove it, look closely, and put it back.

1
Disconnect any PCIe power cables from the GPU (the 6 or 8-pin connectors)
2
Unscrew the small screw at the bracket on the back of the case
3
Press and HOLD the PCIe release clip at the far end of the slot — it's spring-loaded
4
Gently rock the GPU side to side to release it — never yank straight up
5
Look for: VRAM chips (small black squares), fan blades, video ports (HDMI, DisplayPort)
6
Reinstall: lower it in, press until the clip clicks, reattach power cable
7
💬 Ask AI: 'Why does AI need a GPU? What makes it different from a CPU?'

RAM Upgrade
Intermediate · Hardware

RAM is the workspace — the more you have, the bigger the AI model you can run. Practice removing and upgrading.

1
Find the RAM slots — there are usually 2 or 4 colored slots
2
Press BOTH latches at the same time to pop a stick out (it pops up at an angle)
3
Look at the label: DDR3? DDR4? How many GB?
4
Look at the notch on the connector — it won't fit backward (that's a safety feature!)
5
Reinstall: line up the notch, press down firmly until BOTH latches click back in
6
Dual channel tip: for maximum speed, matching sticks go in slots 1+3 (or 2+4)
7
💬 Ask AI: 'How much RAM do I need to run a 7B parameter AI model?'

Full Reassembly Challenge
Advanced · Hardware

Put the whole machine back together from scratch. If it boots, you win.

Order matters: CPU → thermal paste (pea-sized dot) → cooler → RAM → storage → GPU

If nothing happens: check front panel power connector and reseat RAM
